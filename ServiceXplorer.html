FEATURES

ServiceXplorer is a tool for business service monitoring, integrating components from different scopes but having some kind of dependency or relationship that can impact the response time or the service availability. ServiceXplorer is composed of microservices and modules running asynchronously through Django, Kafka, Sqlalchemy, MongoDB, PostgreSQL, and in the frontend, Javascript and Jquery.

Introduction
 Have you ever thought about the number and types of components that business services in your organization depend on to function? Don't forget to add to all this heterogeneity the volume of instances, containers, microservices, applications, and dozens of other components in the cloud or on-premises.

With the implementation of a DevOps model, development and operations teams began to use practices to automate processes that historically have always been manual and slow. Now, with integration and continuous delivery mechanisms, the process of launching solutions, from the creation phase to the implementation phase, has made the management of dynamic and complex systems more effective and with less risk.
At the same time, new real-time management trends, capable of glancing these different components in a more integrated way at different stages of development, and how much business segments are impacted, have become targets of new monitoring approaches.

In fact, what we need can no longer be provided by just traditional monitoring of infrastructure, database, applications or inciden​ts generated by resource planning sws. A change in approach, favoring the construction of operational business centers, looking at the components of a service in an integrated way, establishing rules for status propagation and other dependency relationships, will enable a unified and more practical view of the service maps.   How to monitor environments with such diversity and requirements? How to define, for example, the real impact on a specific service after an increase in I/O load in a given workload ? What actions should be taken if a service that depends on the delivery of products, receives an alert of an unexpected delay in one of its components ?
Observability and AIOPS, although important mechanisms in noise elimination, incident correlations, data normalization, grouping and analysis for predictability, still require agile methods and more effective diagnoses, when pointing out root causes of problems related to availability and performance of business services. 

ServiceXplorer is an alternative that aims to fill this gap, providing a practical alternative for organizing and monitoring business services, taking into account the plethora of component items involved in their compositions.  This is a brief presentation, whose purpose is to describe how the ServiceXplorer solution was conceived, the control mechanisms used and the main components that were part of its development.

Objectives
Basically, in a nutshell, ServiceXplorer creates service maps using a relationship structure based on the dependency between the most diverse component items composing these services, allowing to establish how the incident severities from these components impact the availability and the service performance, and consequently, generate a more accurate and agile diagnosis in the location of service offenders.  Observability and AIOPS, although important mechanisms in noise elimination, incident correlations, data normalization, grouping and analysis for predictability, still require methods more effective when pointing out root causes of problems inside a business services perspective. 

Basics

We will start by exposing the main components that govern ServiceXplorer, covering some concepts that are the basis for its formation.   

Tree Structure
According to Rachel Lebeaux, senior managing editor for SearchCompliance.com, Business Services are referred to as the activities that assist business yet does not deliver a tangible commodity. A Business Service includes one or more technical services that provide value to internal or external customers, and usually includes an associated Service Level Agreement (SLA) that specifies the terms of the service. For instance, Information Technology is one such business service that supports various other business services such as shipping, procurement and finance.

In a nutshell, we can say about Business Services as being composed by some business processes, monitored through specific business metrics and supporting services an organization offers its customers. The goal of business process monitoring is to extract business events that refer to a well-defined step in the business process activity from transaction logs, which in turn are based on the trace information provided by transactional metrics captured from Application Layer. Application Layer, composed by applications built over microservices or monolithic architecture.It is critical that we are able to map component items and how they relate to each other to perform the transactions required by business processes. In the Application Layer, we must define these components and their dependency relationships in a hierarchical model. Even considering that architectures based on microservices naturally have mesh interconnections, it is essential to translate the dependency relationships into a hierarchical model, since that is the model supported by this ServiceXplorer version.                                                                                                                    

Walking through the hierarchical model, we reach the Infrastructure Layer.  The Infrastructure Layer or Device Service Layer refers all physical or virtual devices, instances, containers, databases, processes and component items, for which are sent status information about traffic, bandwidth, and other sources of resource contentions. One of the great challenges for the construction of the hierarchical tree of services is to map the dependencies of the components present in the Application Layer with the infrastructure resources of the Infrastructure Layer, that is, how the transactions are correlated and  impacted by the resource pool. present in the Infrastructure Layer. As can be seen, our concern should be focused on assessments that can establish dependency relationships between the component items, both in an intra-tier perspective and in an inter-tier view.

While modeling business service, it is possible create a custom Service Model based upon how your organization defines its structure, following an "N-layers" model, with multiple nested service levels,  enabling you to create service hierarchies with a custom number of layers that accurately reflect your service structures within the organization.

Within ServiceXplorer's 3-Layer model, it is possible to include in the Business Services Layer, different layers or sublayers composed of a set of interdependent services. Thus, an online sales service composed of other services such as customer registration services, inventory control, electronic invoicing, distribution and logistics, , must all be grouped within the Business Service layer.  In the other layers, the same modeling criterion should be adopted in all other layers

As will be described later, ServiceXplorer offers a set of REST APIs that allow external monitoring tools to send the status of monitored components such as services, applications and infrastructure. Likewise, ServiceXplorer also provides REST APIs designed to create and update relationship maps between component items. This will allow ServiceXplorer to be integrated with CMDB tools, for example,  and consequently bring more automation and efficiency to the service tree assembly process.

Tree Components

Before proceeding further, defining how ServiceXplorer  should operate in the service tree, let's talk about some concepts and rules that must be observed when building the hierarchical structure.


Parent, Child, and Sibling Nodes

Since the dependency relationship between the componentes  is based on a hierarchical structure, it is necessary highlight definitions, levels and functions of each node or component, over this mapping. As can be seen in the figure below, , any subnode of a given node is called a child node, and the given node, in turn, is the child’s parent. Sibling nodes are nodes on the same hierarchical level under a common parent. Nodes  in a highest level than nodes in the same lineage are ancestors and those below it are descendants.



Leaf Nodes

End nodes or leaf nodes (which I will call nodes), are associated with components managed by some monitoring tool capable of generating notifications and alerts. Nodes are primary web services that provide an ability to receive incidents sent by these external management tools, and translate them into the service tree in terms of the degree of severity and impact that this component can have on the servicesof interest.


It is noteworthy that the severity level of an incident, caused by a failure or problem in a component, will not necessarily have similar impact in the service context. As an example, a failure considered critical in some component related to the infrastructure of a server does not mean that the same degree of criticality has to be attributed to the service. Although this server is a key component for the service availability, it is possible that the service has server redundancy and, therefore, that server downtime does not compromise the service unavailability.


In the case of subnodes, they are not directly associated with the incidents generated by the monitoring tools. The use of subnodes in the service tree is related to some kind of functional grouping of nodes. Basically, subnodes are created taking into account the grouping category of their children.

The first type of grouping is based on the composition of child nodes. This is commonly seen in situations where the existence of a component (subnode) is only possible with the presence of other components (nodes). Servers and VMs are examples of components created by the composition of other nodes represented by infrastructure components, like cpu, memory, network links p.e.

The second category of node grouping is based on indirect dependency relationships. In this case, although the subnode is not composed of child nodes, as infrastructure components, it still depends on child nodes for its full operation.  A classic and very simple example to understand would be the relationship between processes and operating system. Although a process (subnode) is not composed of the operating system (child node), problems on OS internal modules, can compromise the availability of processes running on this server.   Although it is not recommended, you can create a hybrid subnode containing some nodes, which it is composed of, and other nodes, which it only depends on for its full operation.

Non-Leaf Nodes 

As seen at the example in the next figure, the hierarchical tree branch, has 2 subnodes, database and processes, the latter being also a child node of the database subnode. In case of a malfunction of any of the child nodes there will be a possible reflection on the status of these subnodes, but it may happen that the scope of the problem in the child node does not interfere with the parent component (subnode) functioning. Since an Operating System (OS) has different layers, a functional or performance incident in any of these layers will not necessarily impact the status of processes related to the database, although there is a significant probability.

In the figure you will also notice that the database subnode is composed of volumes, processes and net connections, while the process only depends on the OS.

On the other hand, you can build hybrid service trees, containing both types.


Component Status

Severity status or simply status of a component item, denotes the current state of a component, from an operational or performance perspective. A component exhibiting the highest level of severity means that it no longer responds for the function it should, as opposed to the normal severity status, the lowest level of severity, where the resulting KPIs are within the expected SLA.

It is worth noting that the Unknown status was created by ServiceXplorer to establish the impossibility of defining the severity level for the component, probably either because no information from the incidents, involving the component, were generated by the external monitoring tools or due to an identification error of the component, at the time of its addition or change in the ServiceXplorer . Anyway, components with Unknown status do not propagate iseverity status to the parent node, that is, ServiceXplorer does not consider these components as actives.



Risks and Health Status

Since the leaf nodes in the Device Service Layer  will be supplied with incidents from the external monitoring mechanisms, it will be necessary for ServiceXplorer to validate these incidents, transform them into severity status and send the result to their parent nodes, for which some the rules will also apply in calculating their status before sending them to their respective parent nodes, and so on. ServiceXplorer provides the following options for setting severity levels:









  • arithmetic mean: mean value of the severity of the child nodes
  • root mean: root mean square of the severity of the child nodesmple Item 2
  • most critical: most critical severity of child nodes (default)
  • less critical: less critical severity of child nodes
  • highest number of occurrences: severity with the highest number of occurrences  among the child nodes
  • lowest number of occurrences: severity with the highest number of occurrences among the child nodes

Taking into account the figure on the side, the following values could  be associated with the parent ode, according the severity status rules:

  • arithmetic mean: Warning
  • root mean: Critical
  • most critical: Critical
  • less critical: Normal
  • highest number of occurrences: Critical
  • lowest number of occurrences: Normal. In this case, take the lower severity level

Status Propagation

Status propagation rules define how a component should transmit its severity to its parent node. Therefore, by using status propagation, it’ll be possible define or alter the effect of a component over the parent node status. Currently, the rule to be used can be one of the following:

one level up: If a component is interpreted as being more important than other sibling components, its severity status is increased by a level. For example, if a component has a severity status as warning, and its status propagation is defined as one level up, its severity status is increased, and so changed from warning to critical level.

one level down: If a component is interpreted as being less important than other sibling components, its severity status is decreased by a level. For example, if a component has a severity status as warning, and status propagation is defined as one level down, its severity status is decreased from warning to normal level

current: If a component propagates its severity status unchanged to its parent node. This is the default.

equal to: The status of the component is set to a specified severity level. The new severity status can be either higher or lower than the original status.

ignore: The severity of the component not influence the parent severity status. 

Weighting

Weighting enables you to associate a factor with a component, and consequently affects its status calculation in relation to other sibling components. However, the weight is not taken as an absolute number but as a fraction of all components  on the same hierarchical level and having a common parent. 

Let's show the calculation performed by ServiceXplorer when applying the arithmetic mean rule to get the severity status of a parent node. First of all, we must consider the values of normal, warning and critical status as 1, 2 and 4 respectively, and their calculation ranges as indicated in the figure on the side. 

Now, consider the following example, where are shown 2 scenarios. 

In the first scenarion , all child nodes have status propagations set as "current" and weights set to the default value (1) and their parent node has its status rule set as arithmetic mean. Taking in account these values, we conclude that the final parent node status  should point to a warning severity level. Why ? Since the weight is equal for all nodes, ServiceXplorer simply adds up all the status values of the child nodes and divides by the total number of child nodes.   But note that this sum of status values must be grouped by weight.

Σ weight x ((number of normal child nodes) x 1 + (number of warning child nodes) x 2 + (number of critical child nodes) x 4)) / total number of weights

After applying the above formula, ServiceXplorer will place the obtained value in the severity calculation range to finally obtain the final status. In our example, the value obtained was 2.33 and consequently, the status set to warning.

Now, consider the second scenario, where the weight of one of the child nodes (child 3) has changed, migrating from 1 to 2. The new weight of child node 3 is causing its share to increase from 33% to 50% of the final calculation, and a decrease from 33% to 25% for the remaining child nodes. We would achieve the same effect if the weight of all nodes were kept at their default value, and one more child node was added, presenting a critical status, like child node 3. Returning to the application of the formula for calculating the parent node status, for this new scenario, we would have:

(1 (1 + 2) + 2(4))/4 = 2.75

thus, leading to a critical severity range, and consequently establishing a new parent node status to critical.

Configuring values ...

After defining the rules and fundamentals that govern each component, it's time to foster  the creation and the service tree assembly. Each component is a cell that stores information about its own configuration and its current status. As will be seen later, the attributes or configuration data are entered during the creation or modification of the component, either using an interactive interface through the ServiceXplorer console, or using the REST API of the API Gateway ServiceXplorer.

Currently, ServiceXplorer console offers specific Sections where the set of attributes of a component can be defined:

Component Identity and Conditions

Section that defines the component identity and whether its monitoring depends on other components. It consists of the following fields:

- name: Component name. Components with unique names are only required when they are sibling nodes, that is, they have a common parent node in the service tree.

In the following figure, taking the selected branches of a hypothetical service tree, it is possible to visualize within the Device Service Layer, a single esx server, hosting vms associated with the web and database services, and generating, as a result, components of a same name, although in different contexts. In the first example, after receiving a critical incident from a external toll, related to the esx server infrastructure (“Infrastructure esx server A”), ServiceXplorer performs the mapping and applies the new status to the “metrics & errors" component. Since there are two components with the same identity, the incident is applied to both components.


In this next scenario, although the incident generated by the monitoring tool is associated with the component "network ports", and there are two components having a common identity, ServiceXplorer is able to define the context over which the status propagation should be applied.

In order to define the context and how to apply severity levels and status propagation rules to the correct components,  ServiceXplorer  uses a component identification based on the component path name. The values of the path names are, in truth, correspond to the paths of the components in the service tree. When configuring components through the  ServiceXplorer  console, the path name  value is automatically assigned to the component. By the other side, In case of alteration, removal or addition of components through API Gateway ServiceXplorer, the path name value must be provided, if there are other components with the same name. Under this circunstance, as will be seen later, the monitoring tools must send inside the payload of communication packets, values corresponding to the name and the path keys. If thepath name is not sent,  ServiceXplorer  will apply the alert to all Service Tree components that have the same name. In a nutshell,  ServiceXplorer uses name and path name, if necessary, to uniquely identify components in the service tree.

  - description : Component description. It is optional.

Under  Conditions  it is possible to define restrictions and dependencies on the behavior of the components of the service tree. Only an unique condition can be set on each component:


 


  • unconditional: there is no dependency on other components besides the child components in the hierarchical structure (default);
  • active  components:   the current component will be in active state in the service tree, only if the referenced components are part of the hierarchical structure, and are in a non-unknown status;
  • inactive  components: the current component will be active in the service tree, only if the referenced components are part of the hierarchical structure, and are in an unknown status;

In case one of the two options is used, either active components or inactive components,  the components on which the current component is dependent must be selected from a list containing all the components of the service tree. At this point, it is good to remember that, incidents generated by  monitoring tools  addressing  components with unknown status will have no effect on them and their parent nodes.

-  location: Optional field which describes the component location, physical or virtual (p.e. container, aws, datacenter...).

- type: Optional field that defines the component type and helps to visualize the component in the services tree. If the component type is not within the list offered by ServiceXplorer, a new type can be added by simply downloading a new icon that represents the desired type, on the contrary, ServiceXplorer will use a default type to graphically represent the component.


Status and Propagation

Section that defines the component's status, based on the criticality of their child components in the service tree, and at the same time, how its status should be propagated to the parent component.

- severity level :  Defines the component status calculation, taking into account the status of the child nodes, in the case of parent nodes, or just the severity reported by the external monitoring tool, as already covered in Risks and Health Status.

- propagation status :  Define how the status of the current node will be propagated to the parent node, except for the service tree root node. Their values and what they mean, can be checked in Status Propagation section.

- weight : Integer value that designates the multiplicative factor to apply when calculating the status of any node except the root node. More details, in Weight section.


Operability and Maintenance

Section that defines the intervals reserved for maintenance of components, leaving them in an unknown status during this time window. All incidents sent by external tools, , and related to the selected components, will not be processed. If the entire service tree, including all its components, has to be placed under maintenance, just select the root component of the service tree.  

- maintenance : define date, time and intervals when the component will not be in active state. 


Automatic Actions

Sextion that provides integration with ITSM tools or other notification tools, as soon as the component reaches a condition previously established. The fields in this section are optional:

- condition : define the component status condition to trigger a notification. One or more option can be selected of the list: normal, unknown, warning and critical.

- command : defines the command to be executed by ServiceXplorer. The variables $name and $condition, regarding the component name and the component status, respectively, can be used as arguments by the integration program. Here's a note. For the execution of a notification command, ServiceXplorer creates a thread, without waiting for its execution to finish. The thread, in turn, triggers the command and waits for its completion, because in addition to obtaining the status of the command's execution, the thread needs to read the output generated by the command, especially if this output is the identification of an open ticket, in case the notification command is an integrator itsm. The ticket ID is stored by ServiceXplorer and allows the integrator program to reference this ID in future calls (such as during a ticket closing operation). This ticket ID can be accessed by the notification command through the $ticket variable.

Architecture

The architecture is centered on the Django web framework and its Model-View-Template (MVT) pattern. The ServiceXplorer project is deployed over WSGI and all requests are, on a first hand, handled by the Apache web server, before being forwarded and resolved by views. Views have been created to address specific web requests: incidents coming from monitoring tools, users asking for changes in the service tree, by using command line or browser interfaces, and periodic heartbeats coming from client browsers for checking status and relationship changes.The proposed architecture aims to address the following concerns:
        - Services are decoupled, fungible, and independent
        - Messages are buffered and consumed when resources are available
        - Services scale easily for high-volume event processing
        - Component are highly available and fault-tolerant
In this way, the solution offers a viable alternative for real-time updating of the status of components and independence of the rate of incidents, providing adaptability mechanisms (horizontal and vertical) that can be used dynamically, according to the volume of information.  

The ServiceXplorer components used in the solution are limited to Apache Web Server, Web Server Gateway Interface, Django, Apache Kafka Server, SQL Alchemy, PostgreSQL, JQuery and a set of modules and controls written in Python and Javascript.



At this moment, Kafka Cluster has been configured in 2 brokers, 8 partitions and a replication factor of 2. In the next release, to better address aspects such as scalability and fault tolerance, connectors and streams will be created, relying on Kafka ksqlDB for the processing the messages, aggregating and transforming data in real time, before generating output streams to be used for notification and update of the status of the service tree nodes.

Handling Incidents

Let's start by talking about the flow of tasks triggered during the reception of an incident sent by an external monitoring tool. The figure below is a summary of the different interactions between the ServiceXplorer components present in the flow.

Incidents sent by external tools to ServiceXplorer, using the RESTful API in HTTP POST requests, are handled by a specific view in the Django context, whose main objective is to validate the request, ensuring that all keys and their respective values, formats and data types sent in the package body, are valid, as well as all fields considered mandatory are present. Also during this step, the validator accesses the service tree database to make sure that the component, referenced in the incident, is a valid component, that is, it is part of the service tree and has an active monitoring status.  In the figure above, a snippet of the json structure sent by the external monitoring tool to ServiceXplorer is shown. Here are some comments worth mentioning. The customer key can be related to a customer, an area or a sector of a company. The name key in the "component" key corresponds to the parent node of the component represented by the name key inside "subcomponents". If the parent node identity is absent, ServiceXplorer will associate the incident with all components with the same identification of the name key in "subcomponents". It is also worth noting that more than one incident, relating to more than one service tree, can be sent within a single packet.

After this initial check, the validation function returns an HTTP response, with a status code defining success or failure of the validation process, however, without waiting for the incident to be processed at all stages of the processing flow. It is noteworthy that the Django and Kafka follow a asynchronous model in most of processing and  communication between the components present in the ServiceXplorer  architecture. 

If the incident validation process is successful, the validator calls a async view responsible for publishing the incident using the message broker (Kafka). The topic "Incident" is created with a message describing the incident, the involved component and the event status. The getIncident microservice, the subscriber of the "Incident" topic, consumes the message containing the incident details. and performs the following actions in sequence:
        - Establishes the component identity and the incident status
        - Queries the current status for this component in the service tree database, as well as the location of that component and its parent identification in the hierarchical structure. Since a component can be present at different places in the service tree, more than one record can be returned from this query.
        - If the incident status is different from the component's current severity, the component's status is updated in the service tree database, otherwise the processing of the following steps is aborted
        - For each hierarchical level related to the location of the component, obtained by consulting the service tree database, the getIncident microservice publishes a corresponding message, containing the parent component identification, and a transaction tag. In this publication, the topic used is identified as "level-<value-1>", where <value> is the number corresponding to the hierarchical level of the component in the service tree. For each hierarchical level related to the location of the component, obtained by consulting the service tree database, the getIncident microservice publishes a corresponding message, with the parent component identification, and the transactional tag. ​Supposing,  that a component is present in two hierarchical levels of the tree, level 7 and level 3 for example, two topics will be created, one identified as "level-6" and the other one as "level-2" , respectively. The message content addressed to these two different message queues will have the identification of the corresponding parent in each hierarchical level. 

For each topic "level-<value-1>", at least one subscriber, represented by the "level-<value-1>" microservice, will be responsible for the consumption and processing of the message. Basically, a Level microservice includes the following tasks:
        - Information from the service tree database regarding the status and identification of the parent component of the component referenced in the message, as well as all its child nodes, including the status, weight and propagation rules are consulted.
        - The new value of the component status is calculated, and if different from the component's current severity, the component status is updated in the service tree database, otherwise the processing of the following steps is aborted.
        - The "level-<value-1>" microservice publishes a message, containing the parent component identification, and the original transactional tag. In this publication, the topic used is identified as "level-<value-2>".  

This queuing process follows the same flow until the lowest level of hierarchy is reached, that is, the root node of the services tree, where the figure of a parent component does not exist.  Note that the "level-<value-i>" microservice is both subscriber of the topic "level-<value-i>" and publisher of the topic "level-<value- (i + 1)>", with exception to the getService (or "level-1") microservice, which does not have the publish function.

Database Access

Currently, ServiceXplorer is working with SQLAlchemy ORM in order to facilitate the translation of Python classes from or to tables, move data between instances of those classes, and provide a standard interface where code creation becomes independent of the database in use. All modules and microservices, without exception, communicating with MongoDB (NoSQL) and PostgreSQL through the SQLAlchemy ORM library.

Besides the communication between the Level microservices, Incident microservice and Service microservice, based on Message Broker Kafka, another special microservice is part of the ServiceXplorer architecture, whose function is to store the history of all interactions that occurred, assisting in tracking transactions and controlling the activities of these ServiceXplorer objects. That database microservice subscribes to all the topics discussed above. The transactional tag field is read  from the message body, and used by the database microservice, as the primary key during operations on the history log table. Transactional tags allow you to identify and track all tasks performed while processing each incident received from the monitoring tools.

Throughout the project, other actions performed by the db microservice in database update functions, will also be seen, , either acting on the so-called metadata in MongoDB or on final data in the PostgreSQL relational tables. Therefore, all database operations performed by any element of the ServiceXplorer architecture are based on the SQLAlchemy ORM library, using two dialects: the MongoDB and PostgreSQL engines.

MongoDB stores metadata for all objects of the service tree. This includes customer, service and component identification, component configuration values, such as propagation and status rules, weight, type, creation and modification dates, parent identification, and other control information. On the other hand, PostgreSQL relational database stores the status or criticality of service tree components, as well the changes occurring in the service tree over time (addition, removal and alteration of components, component severity changes). 

PostgreSQL also stores information about active sessions. During user login process, ServiceXplorer  creates a session and from there, all communication between frontend and backend is uniquely identified through this session number. The session table in PostgreSQL stores the session owner, session status (active or not active), access mode (edit or read-only), current service tree, last session heartbeat datetime, and operations pending. Pending operations are those where the backend has not yet sent its results to the frontend, or those where the backend has already processed them and sent their results to the frontend and is waiting for a return from the frontend, before the transaction is complete.

After the login step and a new session has been established, the user must open a services tree. At this point, the server should query the two databases in MongoDB and PostgeSQL, to look up the tree structure and get the current state of its components, respectively. Using those information,  ServiceXplorer creates an image of the service tree in a json structure and returns it to the frontend, where the tree is then displayed

From there, all other interactions between foreground and background are carried out through the so-called asynchronous heartbeat messages.


Frontend to Backend Communication


Communication between the frontend and backend modules is always initiated by the frontend and is based on small packets sent within a periodicity established  via the keepalive configuration parameter.. In their simplest form, these packets, also called heartbeats, contain in their payloads the identification of the session and the service tree identification, currently active on the user's station.

After receiving the heartbeat packets from the frontend, the Django view queries the service tree database, looking for changes in the status of any component of the service. If there have been changes, only the components involved in these changes and their new status are pushed back to the frontend.

The frontend gets the web response from the backend, and if there are changes to be implemented in the hierarchical tree graphical interface, a new heartbeat packet is sent to the backend, confirming that the changes received in the last packet were successfully applied by the frontend. If the return of heartbeat packets sent by the backend does not indicate any change in the service tree, the frontend only returns an ack After the backend receives this "confirmation" from the frontend, Django view should remove the record from the database, through db microservice, if there are no open sessions having the same service active.  That is, as long as there are sessions with the same service open and active, the update records referring to the status of its components will only be removed from the service tree database, after the last session returns an acknowledgment to the last update record sent . If the frontend does not send an ack packet, the records with this information will remain in the database, and the backend will try to resend this information in the next heartbeat package received from the frontend with the same service id.

Service tree updates are not restricted to changes in the  status of its components. Sessions can also request structural changes to the tree, with the addition or removal of components, for example.  To make these types of changes, sessions send the intended changes in the same heartbeat packet in the form of a json structure. It is noteworthy here that these requests cannot originate only from interactive sessions, where the user, using the ServiceXplorer graphical interface, executes editing and control commands. ServiceXplorer provides application programming interfaces (APIs) that allow users to programmatically manage different tree structures using POST requests.   Regardless of the method used, whether interactively through sessions or programmatically with APIs, tree change requests for the same service can only be served one at a time.  ServiceXplorer does not support concurrent edit operations on the same service tree. In the case of structural changes to a service tree, the metadata stored in MongoDB are changed and not just the records in PostgreSQL. Those changes will be sent for all sessions having the  same service tree open, through the return of the heartbeat packets.


Users and Sessions

ServiceXplorer  offers two user profiles. The administrator, in addition to the functions of changing and creating new service trees, can also view the progress of management of the business and infrastructure components. However, even if the administrator has the ability to change the service tree, whether in interactive or programmatic mode, a service tree can only be edited for a single session. Considering some users with administration profile and having open sessions in a specific tree of services, only one user-session will have the right to edit this tree. Thus, if an administrator requests editing of a service tree,  ServiceXplorer  will fulfill the request only if no other session has the same service tree in edit mode. The administrator with the session in editing mode can return to read mode in an explicit way, interactively using controls on the ServiceXplorer page, or implicitly, if the user closes the session or the session heartbeat is not received by the nackend within a pre-time interval. set (changeable via the  ServiceXplorer  parameter configuration page). In addition to the administrator profile, ServiceXplorer offers the operator profile for those users who will only have the right to read the service trees.

In addition to reading mode and editing mode, a session from any user profile. can put the service tree in active or non-active mode. In active mode, structural or status changes to any of its components will be reflected in the ServiceXplorer page, while in non-active mode these changes will not be seen in the session. Here's an observation. Configuring a session in active or non-active mode, although from a session point of view it has the same effect, is different from the situation where the service tree is put under maintenance (reference: Section Operability and Maintenance). In the latter case, although the monitoring tools report incidents and indicate the current status of monitored components, ServiceXplorer will ignore them without any information being processed in this maintenance period, meaning that all active and non-active sessions will not reflect any updates to the service tree in this period. On the other hand, in case the session is placed in non-active mode by the user, only this specific session will not display any service tree update, and all other currently active sessions in this same period will reflect the changes reported by the tools external monitoring. It is recommended that  a service tree put into edit mode by an active session, has other sessions in non-active mode until the edit is complete.

As already mentioned in Frontend to Backend Communication section, the keepalive parameter defines the time in seconds defining the interval that the heartbeat packet is sent to the backend. If a session does not send a heartbeat within this interval, ServiceXplorer releases all data structures held in the backend regarding that session and returns an error to the frontend. Unless the tree is reopened, commands executed by the session will have no effect. The default is 60 and the minimum value is 30. When creating a user, keepalive is automatically set to its default value. In this way, all sessions opened by the user will have their heartbeat packets sent every 60 seconds, unless the administrator or the user who owns the session changes its value.


Dockerizing ServiceXplorer

ServiceXplorer is based on five services, created and managed through Docker Compose. 

The django-httpd service was deployed inside the container running Apache, which is used for reverse proxy to forward requests either to Django, via mod_wsgi, or the static file directory. PostgreSQL, MongoDB, Kafka and more the set of microservices are part of the other services that make up the ServiceXplorer application. All containers initially run on a single VM. Although ServiceXplorer is based on swarm mode, no swarm cluster has been configured and therefore swam is a single node. On the other hand, in this type of configuration, it will be possible to grade the scalability of services by adding new workers, taking into account several factors such as the number of active users, volume and size (number of components and sublayers) of the service trees, and the values used for the keepalive parameter.
